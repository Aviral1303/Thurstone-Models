\begin{abstract}
Large-scale preference leaderboards such as Chatbot Arena rank language
models by maximum-likelihood Bradley--Terry (BT), whose logistic link
assumes independence of irrelevant alternatives across a comparison pool
that never stops growing. We test whether a flexible Thurstonian
alternative---a lattice-based model that on pairwise data reduces to a
steeper, natively tie-aware gap link---measurably improves refit
stability, held-out calibration, or tie handling on 1.67 million Arena
votes spanning 129 models and sixteen months. Every comparison was
pre-registered: effect-size thresholds derived before fitting, synthetic
detection gates passed before real data, verdict criteria fixed in
advance. We find no demonstrable advantage in either direction. Refit
stability under population growth is statistically indistinguishable
(median paired rank-correlation difference $+0.00000$ across thirteen
month-over-month refits); two structurally different tests agree that link
shape is not discriminable at the ability gaps real matchups occupy
(analytic ceilings of $0.23$--$0.31\times$ our minimum practical
difference); and the calibration and tie-mechanism comparisons both end
\emph{inconclusive}, each dominated by a single-window episode rather than
any systematic difference. Inside this null sit two structural findings: a
steeper link's single tie parameter entangles tie mass with decisive
discrimination, whereas Davidson's tie parameter is provably orthogonal to
its logistic link; and under parameter uncertainty, steep links trade
prediction-side overconfidence against an estimation-side shrinkage that
once protected against a severe cold-start extrapolation error. One
anomaly survives every attribution attempt: temporally localized excess
dispersion under every link tested. Practically, cold-start coverage and
nonstationary drift---not link choice---bind next-month predictability in
deployments of this shape.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}

Chatbot Arena~\citep{chiang2024arena} has become the de facto public
scoreboard for large language models: users pose a prompt, two anonymous
models respond, and the user votes for a winner or declares a tie. The
platform converts these votes into a leaderboard by maximum-likelihood
Bradley--Terry (BT)~\citep{bradleyterry1952}, fitting one latent ability
per model under a logistic link. Our study window alone contains 1.8
million recorded battles across 129 models (1.67 million after the
platform's own deduplication filter), with new models entering the
comparison pool continuously throughout.

BT buys its simplicity with a strong structural assumption. As a
logit-family choice model it satisfies Luce's independence of irrelevant
alternatives~\citep{luce1959}: the odds of model $A$ beating model $B$ are
taken to be invariant to whatever else occupies the pool. Nothing about
human preference data guarantees this, and a leaderboard whose pool grows
by the month---forcing a global re-estimate over an ever-changing
comparison graph---is precisely the setting where one might expect the
assumption to bite: incumbent ratings could shift when entrants arrive,
predicted win probabilities could miscalibrate for fresh models, and the
tie outcome (roughly a fifth of quality votes) is not even part of BT's
native outcome space. The Thurstonian random-utility
family~\citep{thurstone1927} offers the classical alternative---each model
draws a latent performance and the better draw wins---and Cotton's
lattice construction~\citep{cotton2021} makes that family computationally
exact for arbitrary base densities, with ties arising natively as
dead-heat mass rather than by parametric extension~\citep{davidson1970}.
On pairwise data the lattice model reduces to a \emph{gap link}: a
probability curve in the ability difference, like BT's logistic but with
a different shape---steeper at a toss-up (slope $0.284$--$0.337$
depending on its tie-band width, against the logistic's $0.25$) and
carrying a native tie band. Whether that different shape is worth
anything in a real deployment is an empirical question, and to our
knowledge it had not been asked at this scale.

We asked it four ways, each pre-registered before any real-data number
was computed: practical-significance thresholds derived from
deployment-meaningful error sizes, detection gates validated on synthetic
ground truth, and pattern-based verdict criteria fixed in advance.
(RQ1)~When new models enter, whose refit perturbs incumbent rankings
less? (RQ2)~Is either link shape more consistent with observed win rates
across triples of models? (RQ3)~Which link calibrates better on held-out
votes, especially for recent entrants? (RQ4)~Does a native dead-heat band
fit the observed tie structure better than Davidson's extension of BT?

The answer, delivered with unusual statistical redundancy, is that the
link does not matter here. Refit stability is indistinguishable---across
thirteen month-over-month refits the median paired rank-correlation
difference is $+0.00000$, with the two methods exactly tied in five
windows---while window-to-window variation in stability, governed by pool
maturity, exceeds the between-method differences by roughly two orders of
magnitude. Two structurally different analyses (an
analytic calibration ceiling and an empirical triple-additivity test)
independently agree that at the ability gaps real matchups occupy, no
plausible member of the Thurstonian family is distinguishable from the
logistic. The two remaining comparisons, held-out calibration and tie
handling, both end \emph{inconclusive} rather than equivalent---a
distinction we maintain deliberately, because each verdict was dominated
by a single anomalous window rather than by any systematic difference,
and our pre-registered criteria refuse a call under exactly that pattern.

This paper's contribution is that negative result, stated with the
precision it deserves, together with what we found inside it. First, a
structural asymmetry: a steep link's single tie parameter entangles tie
mass with decisive discrimination, while Davidson's tie parameter is
provably orthogonal to its logistic link---forcing the lattice band to
absorb both-bad-scale tie mass measurably degrades its decisive
accuracy. Second, a two-faced interaction between link steepness and
parameter uncertainty: steep links make overconfident plugin predictions
from noisy estimates, yet the same steepness acts as implicit shrinkage
in estimation---in our data it once prevented a severe cold-start
extrapolation error that cost BT dearly. Third, an open question: a
temporally localized excess dispersion, present under every link we
tested and attributable to none of the covariates we measured. And
throughout, a deployment lesson that inverts the motivating question:
what binds ranking quality in a system like Arena is not the link
function at all, but cold-start coverage and nonstationary drift---both
of which dwarf every between-method difference we measured. We believe
the audit itself, and the pre-registration discipline that kept four
successive verdicts honest, are as much the contribution as any single
number.

\section{Related work}\label{sec:related}

\paragraph{Paired comparison models.} The random-utility tradition begins
with \citet{thurstone1927}: each alternative draws a latent performance
and the larger draw wins, giving a probit-type link under Gaussian noise
\citep{mosteller1951}. \citet{bradleyterry1952} replaced the probit with
the logistic, which \citet{luce1959} showed is equivalent to the choice
axiom now called independence of irrelevant alternatives (IIA);
\citet{harville1973} and \citet{plackett1975} extend the same
multiplicative structure to multi-entry contests and full rankings, and
\citet{davidson1970} extends BT with an explicit tie outcome. Practical
rating systems descend from the same family: Elo~\citep{elo1978} and
Glicko~\citep{glickman1999} are online approximations to BT-style
updating, and TrueSkill~\citep{herbrich2007trueskill} is a Thurstonian
(Gaussian) model fit by approximate message passing. We position TrueSkill
as a relative only: it shares the Thurstonian generative story but uses
approximate inference and an online update rule, and \emph{we do not test
it empirically here}---our comparison isolates the link-shape question by
holding the estimation machinery fixed, which TrueSkill's different
inference style would confound. Ranking distributions without a latent
performance scale (Mallows-type models and the repeated insertion family;
\citealp{doignon2004}) are a separate tradition; \citet{boehmer2024} warn
that their behavior depends delicately on the number of alternatives,
which is one reason we stay inside the random-utility family throughout.

\paragraph{The lattice model, and what this paper does \emph{not} test.}
\citet{cotton2021} made the Thurstonian family computationally exact for
arbitrary base densities by discretizing performances onto a lattice:
the distribution of the winner of an $N$-entrant field is computed once
in $O(N)$, ties arise natively as dead-heat mass, and the construction is
\emph{coherent under field changes}---entrants can be added or removed
without changing the kind of model being fit. That multi-entrant field
coherence is the paper's headline contribution, and \textbf{nothing in
the present study tests it}: Chatbot Arena votes are pairwise only, and
on two-entrant data the lattice construction reduces to a gap link
(Section~\ref{sec:models})---a one-dimensional probability curve in the
ability difference, exactly comparable to BT's logistic. What we evaluate
is therefore the \emph{link shape and tie mechanism} that the lattice
family induces on pairwise data, with the lattice machinery serving as
the computational vehicle that makes flexible Thurstonian links and exact
dead-heat mass cheap to compute. A test of field coherence itself would
require listwise ($\geq 3$-way) preference data, which no public Arena
release contains.

\paragraph{Arena methodology.} Chatbot Arena's leaderboard
\citep{chiang2024arena} is fit by maximum-likelihood BT over deduplicated
anonymous battles, with ties (of both kinds) contributing half weight to
each direction; our pipeline replicates the published board to a mean
absolute error of $0.18$ Elo points at our data snapshot
(Section~\ref{sec:data}). The platform's methodology has continued to
evolve since our data window---most prominently style-controlled
leaderboard variants that regress out response length and formatting;
our audit uses the raw preference log that the classical leaderboard
consumed, and none of our conclusions concern style adjustment.

\paragraph{Preference inconsistency in LLM evaluation.} A recent line of
work documents violations of the axioms underlying these models when LLMs
are the judges or the judged: positional and permutation biases
\citep{tang2023}, instability that social-choice aggregation is proposed
to repair \citep{potyka2023}, listwise preference objectives that step
beyond pairwise assumptions \citep{liu2024lipo}, and direct measurements
of transitivity and IIA violations \citep{zhao2024}. Our
triple-additivity analysis (Section~\ref{sec:rq2b}) contributes a
human-vote counterpart: a shared, temporally localized violation of
\emph{every} static gap link we test, not attributable to any measured
covariate.

\paragraph{A structural fact we rely on.} Davidson's tie parameter is
exactly orthogonal to its decisive link: conditional on a decisive
outcome, the Davidson model \emph{is} BT for every value of the tie
parameter (Proposition~\ref{prop:davidson}). The lattice family has no
such factorization---its single width parameter controls tie mass and
decisive-link steepness simultaneously. This asymmetry, stated here
because it is a theorem rather than an empirical finding, organizes much
of what Section~\ref{sec:rq4} measures.

\section{Gap-link models: definitions and structure}\label{sec:models}

\subsection{Random utility on pairwise data}

Let each model $i$ carry a latent ability $\theta_i \in \mathbb{R}$. In a
battle between $A$ and $B$, each draws a performance
\begin{equation}
X_A = \theta_A + \varepsilon_A, \qquad X_B = \theta_B + \varepsilon_B,
\qquad \varepsilon_A, \varepsilon_B \stackrel{\text{iid}}{\sim} p,
\end{equation}
and the larger performance wins. Because only the difference matters, all
such models are \emph{gap links}: writing $g = \theta_A - \theta_B$,
\begin{equation}
\Pr(A \succ B) = F(g)
\end{equation}
for some increasing $F$ determined by the noise density $p$. Bradley--Terry
is the special case $F = \sigma$, the logistic
$\sigma(g) = (1+e^{-g})^{-1}$, which is also the unique gap link
satisfying Luce's IIA \citep{luce1959}. A continuous noise density gives
ties probability zero; the two mechanisms below restore them in
structurally different ways.

\subsection{The lattice link: ties as dead-heat mass}

Following \citet{cotton2021}, discretize the noise density onto a uniform
lattice of cell width $u$: performances land on cells
$x_j = j\,u$, $j \in \{-L, \dots, L\}$, with cell probabilities obtained
from a base density (standard normal throughout this paper, scale $1$).
Writing $a_j(g) = \Pr(X_A = x_j \mid g)$ and $b_j = \Pr(X_B = x_j)$, the
three outcome probabilities are
\begin{equation}\label{eq:lattice}
W(g) = \sum_j a_j(g) \Pr(X_B < x_j), \qquad
D(g) = \sum_j a_j(g)\, b_j, \qquad
L(g) = W(-g),
\end{equation}
with $W + D + L = 1$. A tie is a \emph{dead heat}: both performances in
the same cell (Figure~\ref{fig:model}a), so the cell width $u$ is the
tie-band width---$D(0)$ grows with $u$---and the model has exactly one
tie-related parameter, fit by profile likelihood exactly as Davidson's
$\nu$ is. The induced \emph{conditional decisive link} is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:fdec}
F_u(g) \;=\; \frac{W(g)}{W(g)+L(g)},
\end{equation}
plotted in Figure~\ref{fig:model}b. Two properties matter for everything
that follows. First, $F_u$ is \emph{steeper} than the logistic at a
toss-up: $F_u'(0)$ is $0.2899$, $0.3237$, $0.3367$ at
$u \in \{0.1, 0.5855, 0.8\}$, against $\sigma'(0) = 0.25$, and it
\emph{increases} with $u$---carving a wider dead-heat band out of the
middle of the distribution steepens what remains. (As $u \to 0$ the link
approaches the probit-type limit with slope $\approx 0.284$; the
logistic's $0.25$ is unattainable at any width.) Second, and consequently,
tie mass and decisive steepness are controlled by the \emph{same}
parameter: the family cannot adjust one without moving the other. We call
this the \emph{entanglement} property and measure its cost in
Section~\ref{sec:rq4}.

Hatched: dead-heat (tie) mass---the probability both performances land in

\subsection{Davidson: ties by extension, orthogonal by construction}

\citet{davidson1970} extends BT with a tie outcome. In gap form, with
$\nu \geq 0$ and normalizer $Z_\nu(g) = 2\cosh(g/2) + \nu$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:davidson}
\Pr(A \succ B \mid g) = \frac{e^{g/2}}{Z_\nu(g)}, \qquad
\Pr(\text{tie} \mid g) = \frac{\nu}{Z_\nu(g)}, \qquad
\Pr(B \succ A \mid g) = \frac{e^{-g/2}}{Z_\nu(g)}.
\end{equation}

\begin{proposition}\label{prop:davidson}
For every $\nu \geq 0$, the Davidson model's conditional decisive link is
exactly logistic:
$\Pr(A \succ B \mid \mathrm{decisive}, g) = \sigma(g)$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
From Eq.~\eqref{eq:davidson}, conditioning on a decisive outcome cancels
the common denominator:
$e^{g/2} / (e^{g/2} + e^{-g/2}) = (1 + e^{-g})^{-1} = \sigma(g)$.
\end{proof}

Davidson's $\nu$ is therefore \emph{purely} a tie-mass parameter: on
decisive outcomes, Davidson and vanilla BT are the same model, and any
decisive-only comparison between them is vacuous by construction. This is
the exact structural opposite of the lattice's entanglement, and it makes
the two families a clean matched pair for asking whether tie mechanics
have a measurable cost: both have one tie parameter and one ability per
model; they differ only in \emph{where} the tie parameter's influence
stops.

\subsection{Estimation, identifiability, and reporting conventions}

All fits in this paper are per-vote maximum likelihood over individual
battles, by L-BFGS with analytic gradients; only the link differs between
methods. Two likelihood modes are used, matching each experiment's
outcome space. The \emph{half-tie} mode replicates the production
leaderboard's treatment \citep{chiang2024arena}: each decisive vote
contributes weight $2$ to its direction and each tie (of either recorded
kind) weight $1$ to each direction, scored through the conditional
decisive link,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:halftie}
\mathcal{L}_{\text{ht}}(\theta) = \sum_{\text{decisive } (w,\ell)}
2\log F(\theta_w - \theta_\ell)
+ \sum_{\text{ties } (i,j)} \bigl[\log F(\theta_i - \theta_j) + \log
F(\theta_j - \theta_i)\bigr].
\end{equation}
The \emph{native trinomial} mode scores $\{W, D, L\}$ directly and is
used where tie prediction is itself the question
(Section~\ref{sec:rq4}). Abilities are translation-invariant, so every
fit of every method anchors the same reference model,
\texttt{gpt-4-0613}~$= 0$: a fixed dated checkpoint (no silent version
drift), present from the third month of the data onward with high volume,
hence available in every training window---the production anchor
(\texttt{mixtral-8x7b}) enters too late for our earliest refits.
Where ability \emph{magnitudes} are compared across links
(Section~\ref{sec:rq1}), the lattice's are rescaled by
$F_u'(0)/\sigma'(0)$ so that one reported unit buys the same
win-probability change at a toss-up in every method---the exact analogue
of the $400/\ln 10$ constant that turns BT coefficients into Elo points;
we label such numbers convention-dependent wherever they appear. Sign
conventions for the calibration deltas are stated once in each results
section and held fixed.

\section{Data and pre-registered methodology}\label{sec:data}

\subsection{Dataset and pipeline validation}

We use the largest public Chatbot Arena battle log
(\texttt{clean\_battle\_20240814}): $1{,}799{,}991$ anonymous pairwise
battles across $129$ models, timestamped at sub-second resolution from
2023-04-24 to 2024-08-14, with outcomes in $\{$A wins, B wins, tie, tie
(both bad)$\}$; the platform's deduplication filter retains $1{,}670{,}250$
battles, which all fits use. Ties are first-class: $20.45\%$ of
non--both-bad votes over the full period---and drifting, from $13.1\%$ in
the first three months, a fact that shapes several design decisions
below.

Before any comparison, we verified the pipeline reproduces production:
refitting BT at the published leaderboard's own timestamp reproduces the
published ratings to MAE $0.18$ Elo points (Spearman $\rho = 0.99997$,
all $128$ published models). Extending the check across ten published
BT-era snapshots spanning 2023-12 to 2024-08, the era-appropriate
pipeline variant achieves MAE $0.18$--$1.01$ Elo points on nine of ten;
the tenth (2024-04-03) is an unexplained outlier at MAE $2.26$, which we
report rather than drop. As a byproduct the comparison dates the
platform's switch to deduplicated fitting to between the 2024-05-01 and
2024-06-02 snapshots.

\subsection{The pre-registration protocol}\label{sec:prereg}

Every experimental verdict in this paper was fixed before the
corresponding real-data quantity was computed, in four layers.

\textbf{(i) Derived practical-significance thresholds.} Statistical
significance is worthless at $n \approx 10^6$; we instead derive a
\emph{minimum practical difference} (MPD) for each comparison from a
deployment-meaningful error. For decisive calibration (RQ3): a uniform
$10$-Elo-point error in every matchup gap---the granularity at which
leaderboard consumers read ratings---is
$\epsilon = 10 \ln 10 / 400 = 0.0576$ nats, whose expected per-vote
excess log-loss at a toss-up is
\begin{equation}
\tfrac{1}{2}\,p(1-p)\,\epsilon^2
= \tfrac{1}{2}\cdot\tfrac14 \cdot (0.0576)^2 = 4.1\times 10^{-4}
\;\;\Rightarrow\;\; \text{MPD}_{3} = 4\times 10^{-4}\ \text{nats/vote}.
\end{equation}
For trinomial tie calibration (RQ4): a one-percentage-point error in
$\Pr(\text{tie})$ at the observed $\sim\!20\%$ tie level costs
$\tfrac12 \Delta p^2 (1/\bar p + 1/(1-\bar p)) =
\tfrac12 (0.01)^2 (1/0.2 + 1/0.8) = 3.1\times10^{-4}$, so
$\text{MPD}_4 = 3\times10^{-4}$ nats/vote. Differences below MPD are
declared practically equivalent \emph{regardless} of statistical
significance.

\textbf{(ii) Effect ceilings computed before fitting.} Because both links
are fit to the same votes, the largest calibration advantage either
\emph{could} have if its family were exactly true is computable in
advance from the empirical gap distribution alone. With $\hat P$ the
vote-weighted distribution of fitted gaps (median $|g| = 0.31$: real
matchups concentrate near toss-ups) and $F_u$ a lattice truth,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:ceiling}
C \;=\; \min_{s>0}\; \mathbb{E}_{g\sim\hat P}\,
\mathrm{KL}\!\left(\mathrm{Bern}(F_u(g)) \,\big\|\,
\mathrm{Bern}(\sigma(g/s))\right)
\;\leq\; 0.23\,\text{MPD}_3
\end{equation}
across every plausible width and skew, and the trinomial analogue for tie
mechanisms is $\leq 0.31\,\text{MPD}_4$ in \emph{both} truth directions.
These ceilings---established before any held-out vote was scored---mean
the honest a-priori expectation for both calibration comparisons was
practical equivalence, and any verdict exceeding a ceiling would itself
demand a misspecification analysis before a mechanism claim.

\textbf{(iii) Synthetic detection gates.} The full pipeline (fitting,
scoring, pooling, verdict classification) had to pass synthetic
ground-truth gates before touching real outcomes: correct-sign detection
with confidence intervals excluding zero at true effects down to
$0.3\times$MPD; correct positive classification of a $\geq$MPD effect;
no false positive call in any sub-MPD world; and tie-parameter recovery
within $10\%$ in rolling worlds mimicking the real entry schedule. One
gate produced a finding in its own right: in a high-noise synthetic
world, the \emph{correctly specified} steep lattice loses to
misspecified BT held-out ($-1.41\times$MPD)---steep links make
overconfident plugin predictions under parameter uncertainty. Grounding
that noise level in the real data's bootstrap-calibrated standard errors
(Fisher information deflated by the measured factor $0.80$; validated
against the platform's published bootstrap, $\rho = 0.983$) showed the
reversal does \emph{not} operate at real pooled noise---but the mechanism
survives as one face of the steepness asymmetry discussed in
Section~\ref{sec:discussion}.

\textbf{(iv) Pattern-based verdict criteria.} Verdicts are assigned by a
fixed classifier over the per-window results, not by inspection. With
$\bar\Delta$ the vote-weighted pooled mean of per-window mean deltas, CI
its window-cluster bootstrap interval ($B = 10^4$; the effective sample
size is the \emph{number of windows}, $13$, and is stated wherever a CI
appears), and $W^{+}$ the count of windows favoring a method:
\emph{positive} requires $|\bar\Delta| \geq \text{MPD}$, sign consistency
$W^{+} \geq 10/13$ (binomial $p = 0.046$), and a CI excluding zero on the
same side; \emph{equivalence} requires $|\bar\Delta| < \text{MPD}$ with
the CI inside $(-\text{MPD}, +\text{MPD})$---a statistically significant
but sub-MPD lean is reported as ``practically equivalent despite a
detectable directional lean,'' never as a win; anything else is
\emph{inconclusive}, reported as such with the episode that caused it.
Post-hoc analyses are permitted but labeled, answer explicitly narrower
questions, and never override a pre-registered verdict.

\subsection{Experimental grid}

All longitudinal experiments share one grid: cumulative training windows
ending at $14$ monthly checkpoints (2023-07-31 through 2024-07-31, plus
the final published-snapshot cutoff 2024-08-12), giving $13$
train/test window pairs; testing always uses the month following the
training cutoff; models absent from training are unscoreable and counted
(they are $24.8$--$75.8\%$ of decisive test votes per window---a
deployment fact we return to). Where a tie parameter is fit
(Section~\ref{sec:rq4}), it is profiled per window on training data only.
The lattice width is otherwise fixed per experiment at
$u \in \{0.1, 0.5855, 0.8\}$: an arbitrary fine default; the width
profiled on the \emph{first three months only} (look-ahead-free); and the
full-sample profile fit (look-ahead, retained as an upper bound). All
three appear throughout because the ``right'' width is itself
nonstationary---the drift documented in Section~\ref{sec:rq4}.

\section{RQ1: refit stability under population growth}\label{sec:rq1}

\textbf{Question.} When new models enter the pool and the leaderboard
refits, whose incumbent rankings move less? For each adjacent checkpoint
pair we fit both methods on cumulative data at $T$ and $T{+}1$ month,
restrict to incumbents ($\geq 1000$ votes at $T$; an all-models variant
is reported alongside), and compare rankings by Kendall $\tau_b$,
Spearman $\rho$, and maximum rank movement; ability-magnitude drift is
reported in slope-matched units at horizons of one and three months, under
both the common anchor and a median alignment that bounds the anchor's own
noise contribution.

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/fig_rq1.pdf}
\caption{RQ1 headline: month-over-month incumbent ranking stability
(Kendall $\tau_b$, incumbents with $\geq 1000$ votes) for BT and the
lattice link across all thirteen refit windows. The two curves are
statistically indistinguishable---median paired difference $+0.00000$,
with five windows exactly tied---while both improve together as the pool
matures, from $\tau_b \approx 0.97$ to $\approx 0.997$. Source:
\texttt{rq1\_metrics.csv}.}
\label{fig:rq1}
\end{figure}

\textbf{Headline: a null with the signature of genuine equivalence.}
Across the thirteen windows the lattice is better in $5$, worse in $3$,
and \emph{exactly tied} in $5$; the median paired $\tau_b$ difference is
$+0.00000$, and mean $\tau_b$ is $0.98434$ (lattice) versus $0.98358$
(BT). The same picture holds at all three lattice widths, at both
horizons, for both incumbent sets, and for every other stability metric:
no incumbent moved more than five rank positions in any window under
either method, and slope-matched ability drift is $1.73$--$1.77$
Elo-equivalent points per month for every method, all within $0.04$ of
one another. We read the exact ties and dead-zero median as diagnostic:
this is not an underpowered test failing to find a difference---at
$1.67$M votes the estimator differences are simply orders of magnitude
smaller than the quantity being measured.

\textbf{The finding that matters for practice.} Figure~\ref{fig:rq1}
makes the secondary result visible: window-to-window variation in
stability ($\tau_b$ ranging $0.968 \to 0.997$ as the pool matures)
exceeds the between-method differences ($\leq 0.007$, median $0$) by
roughly two orders of magnitude. Stability in a deployment of this shape
is governed by per-model sample size and pool maturity, not by link
family: an operator worried about ranking churn should buy votes for
young models, not a different link. A labeled post-hoc check found no
modulation of stability---or of the (null) method difference---by
entrant count or entrant vote share (all $|\rho| \leq 0.37$, descriptive
$p \geq 0.22$, $n = 13$).

\section{RQ2: link shape via triple additivity}\label{sec:rq2b}

\textbf{Question.} Any gap link makes a sharp overidentifying prediction
on triples. If $\Pr(A \succ B \mid \text{decisive}) = F(\theta_A -
\theta_B)$, then for any three models the inverted head-to-head rates
must add:
$F^{-1}(p_{AC}) = F^{-1}(p_{AB}) + F^{-1}(p_{BC})$. For each dense stable
triple (all three legs $\geq 100$ decisive votes inside a 60-day block;
all three models present throughout the block) we compute the
standardized additivity residual
\begin{equation}\label{eq:zstat}
z \;=\; \frac{F^{-1}(\hat p_{AC}) - F^{-1}(\hat p_{AB}) -
F^{-1}(\hat p_{BC})}{\sqrt{v_{AB} + v_{BC} + v_{AC}}},
\qquad
v_{XY} = \frac{\hat p_{XY}(1-\hat p_{XY})}{n_{XY}\,
\bigl[F'(F^{-1}(\hat p_{XY}))\bigr]^2},
\end{equation}
by the delta method. If the link shape is right and preferences are
stable within the block, $z \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)$ and mean $z^2 \approx
1$; excess mean $z^2$ measures shape misfit in a scale-invariant way, so
different links compete fairly. The data admit $786$ qualifying triples
in five blocks. Synthetic calibration passed (true-link mean $z^2 =
1.02$--$1.03$ at matched leg sizes) and simultaneously established the
test's discrimination limit: in both generating directions the four links
differ by only $0.01$--$0.04$ in mean $z^2$---at real gap scales this
statistic \emph{cannot} tell these links apart, which is itself the
result.

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/fig_rq2b.pdf}
\caption{RQ2: mean squared additivity residual per 60-day block, per
link. All four links move together---between-link differences are within
the test's synthetically measured no-power spread---while two blocks show
a shared excess ($\bar{z^2} = 1.57$ and $1.65$) that no link explains.
Source: \texttt{rq2b\_blocks.csv}.}
\label{fig:rq2b}
\end{figure}

\textbf{Headline: indistinguishable---an independent second ceiling.}
Pooled mean $z^2$ is $1.202$ (logit) versus $1.231/1.252/1.279$ (lattice
widths $0.5855/0.1/0.8$), with the lattice lower in one block of five:
differences of the same order as the no-power spread. This is a second,
independently derived version of the effect ceiling of
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ceiling}: a within-window ranking-consistency statistic with
no held-out scoring, no plugin predictions, and no time splits reaches
the same conclusion as the analytic KL bound---\emph{at the ability gaps
real matchups occupy, no member of this Thurstonian family is
distinguishable from the logistic}. Two structurally different tests
converging is stronger evidence than either alone.

\subsection{The additivity excess: an open question}\label{sec:excess}

The informative structure in Figure~\ref{fig:rq2b} is vertical, not
horizontal: in two specific eras \emph{every} link shows the same excess
dispersion. Blocks 2023-08-22$\to$10-21 and 2023-12-20$\to$2024-02-18
have mean $z^2 = 1.57$ and $1.65$, with $13$--$14\%$ of triples at
$|z| > 2$ against a nominal $4.6\%$; the remaining blocks sit at
$1.04$--$1.37$. Half-block splits localize the first episode to roughly
late September--October 2023 (half-block $z^2 = 2.04$ vs $0.91$) and show
the second persisting undiminished in both halves ($1.74/1.54$)---so
within-window preference drift, which halving would attenuate, is not the
mechanism. Systematic characterization ruled out, in turn: the platform's
deduplication transition (it overlaps only the \emph{cleanest} block);
entry activity (the excess blocks have \emph{fewer} entries, $7$ and
$16$, and lower entrant vote share, $0.62$, than the clean late blocks'
$21$--$22$ and $0.89$--$0.91$---anti-correlated); language mix, judge
counts, votes per judge, tie share, and duplicated-prompt share (none
separates excess from clean blocks); and single-model artifacts (excess
triples span the era's models near base rates). Because the excess is
invariant across every link tested, it is a property of the
vote-generating process, not of any link choice. Two candidate
explanations remain live: \emph{judge-population composition shifts}
(a mixture of judges, each individually consistent, violates additivity
in aggregate) and \emph{genuine context effects} (votes influenced by
what else occupies the pool---the classical IIA-violation mechanism).
Distinguishing them needs per-judge panel structure or a natural
experiment this dataset does not cleanly offer; we state it as an open
question, and note that an entry-based event study---the design we
pre-registered for this purpose---is structurally the wrong instrument
for a phenomenon anti-correlated with entries, which is why we descoped
rather than ran it.

\section{RQ3: held-out calibration of decisive outcomes}\label{sec:rq3}

\textbf{Question and design.} On next month's decisive votes, whose
predicted win probabilities are better calibrated? Both methods are fit
per window in half-tie mode (Eq.~\eqref{eq:halftie}) and scored by log
loss on the shared scoreable decisive votes. Sign convention throughout
this section: $\Delta = \ell\ell_{\text{BT}} - \ell\ell_{\text{lattice}}$
per vote, so positive $\times$MPD favors the lattice. Pre-registered
strata: votes involving a \emph{recent entrant} (first training
appearance within $28$ days of the cutoff) versus established pairs,
with a noise-based pooling of windows whose recent-entrant cohorts were
measurably noisy.

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/fig_rq3.pdf}
\caption{RQ3: per-window mean calibration delta (primary width
$u=0.5855$), in MPD units. Eleven of thirteen windows show sub-practical
BT-leaning deltas inside the equivalence band; a single window carries a
$+5.79\times$MPD excursion. The pre-registered classifier returns
\emph{inconclusive} on exactly this pattern: effect size and sign
consistency disagree. Source: \texttt{rq3\_window\_table\_u0.5855.csv}.}
\label{fig:rq3}
\end{figure}

\textbf{Pre-registered verdict: inconclusive, at all three widths.} The
pooled estimate at the primary width is $+0.25\times$MPD with CI
$(-0.17, +1.22)\times$MPD over $13$ windows: the interval straddles the
practical-significance boundary because $11$--$12$ of $13$ windows lean
BT at sub-practical size while one window (2023-11-30) sits at
$+5.8\times$MPD (Figure~\ref{fig:rq3}). The classifier refuses a call on
this pattern by design, and we report it as designed: \emph{inconclusive
is not equivalent}, and the verdict is hostage to one episode.

\textbf{The episode, mechanistically.} Decomposing the outlier window
(labeled post-hoc): a single model, \texttt{pplx-7b-online}, entered the
pool with \emph{two} training votes at the cutoff. BT's maximum
likelihood extrapolated it to mid-table ($\hat\theta = +0.432$); the
lattice's steeper link---which attains the same win probabilities at
smaller gaps and therefore shrinks low-data models toward the
field---placed it at $+0.004$. The model was mass-sampled the following
month and performed poorly; BT paid $\approx 68$ nats over the window,
$\sim\!80\%$ of the window's entire delta. This is \emph{implicit
shrinkage in estimation}: a real protective effect of link steepness, on
exactly one occasion ($n=1$; we claim the mechanism's existence, not its
frequency).

\textbf{The narrower question, answered.} A labeled post-hoc
decomposition excludes test votes where either model has fewer than $30$
training votes, asking: away from extreme cold-start, are the links
equivalent? The outlier collapses ($+5.8 \to +0.04\times$MPD) and the
verdict becomes \emph{equivalence with a sub-practical BT-leaning
directional lean} at every width: pooled $-0.141 / -0.106 / -0.217
\times$MPD at $u = 0.1/0.5855/0.8$, CIs inside the band and excluding
zero. The lean is largest at the steepest width by a wide margin,
directionally consistent with plugin overconfidence, but it is not
monotone across the two shallower widths---suggestive, not a clean
dose-response. This decomposition answers its own narrower question and
does not override the pre-registered inconclusive verdict above.

\textbf{The strongest single number: theory's best-case location comes
back negative.} The pre-analysis identified the one place a genuine
lattice-truth calibration effect could plausibly clear MPD: the
recent-entrant stratum of the early high-noise windows, where under
lattice-truth the predicted effect was $+1.58\times$MPD. Measured:
$\mathbf{-0.50\times}$\textbf{MPD, CI} $(-1.10, -0.06)$---CI-bearing,
and negative. In its own best-case location, the lattice-truth
hypothesis is not merely unsupported but contradicted. The recent-entrant
stratum overall shows the same sub-practical BT lean as everything else
($-0.12\times$MPD, CI $(-0.18, -0.06)$).

\textbf{What actually limits prediction here.} Two measurements dwarf
every between-method number in this section. First, reliability: fitting
a recalibration slope on held-out predicted logits gives $\approx 1.455$
for \emph{all four} models---every method is underconfident on next
month's votes by the same large factor, a shared nonstationarity that no
link choice addresses. Second, coverage: $24.8$--$75.8\%$ of decisive
test votes per window involve a model with zero training votes and are
unpredictable by \emph{any} ability model. Next-month predictability in
this deployment is bound by cold-start coverage and drift, not by the
link.

\section{RQ4: tie mechanisms}\label{sec:rq4}

\textbf{Question and design.} Does the lattice's native dead-heat band
fit observed tie structure better than Davidson's extension, at equal
parameter count? Both models are fit per window by native trinomial
likelihood, abilities plus one profiled tie parameter each ($\nu$;
$u$), and scored on next month's votes over $\{$A wins, B wins,
tie$\}$. The dead-heat analysis uses the platform's \emph{quality-tie}
label only; ``both bad'' marks two responses below an absolute bar, not
draws that landed close, and is analyzed separately below. Sign
convention: $\Delta = \ell\ell_{\text{Davidson}} -
\ell\ell_{\text{lattice}}$, positive favors the lattice;
$\text{MPD}_4 = 3\times10^{-4}$ nats/vote.

\textbf{Pre-registered verdict: inconclusive.} The pattern mirrors
RQ3's, with a different cause. Pooled $+0.838\times$MPD, CI $(+0.017,
+2.542)$; the lattice is better in $10/13$ windows but the estimate is
carried by the same calendar window as RQ3's outlier
($+9.55\times$MPD). Decomposition, however, refutes the natural guess
that this is the same cold-start event: the \texttt{pplx} pairs
contribute $-2.8\%$ of this window's delta. The driver is the \emph{tie
channel on near-peer, largely same-family pairs}---tie votes contribute
$+407$ nats (lattice better) against $-301$ on decisive votes, led by
\texttt{gpt-4-0314} vs.\ \texttt{gpt-4-1106-preview} and similar sibling
matchups. The two pre-registered calibration comparisons were thus both
driven inconclusive by single-window episodes in the same month through
\emph{different} channels---at effect ceilings of a fraction of MPD,
verdicts are hostage to individual episodes, not systematic link
differences. Consistently, a labeled cold-start filter that resolved
RQ3's outlier barely moves this one ($+0.861\times$MPD, still
inconclusive).

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/fig_rq4_curves.pdf}

\label{fig:rq4curves}

\textbf{The tie-band answer: genuinely different shapes the data cannot
discriminate.} The two fitted mechanisms imply nearly identical at-zero
tie mass in every window ($\Pr(\text{tie}\mid 0)$ within $\sim\!0.01$)
but structurally different bands: the gap at which the tie probability
halves is $2.84$--$2.93$ ability units for Davidson's slow
$\operatorname{sech}$-type decay versus $1.67$--$1.68$ for the lattice's
overlap decay---a stable $\sim\!1.7\times$ difference across all
thirteen windows (Figure~\ref{fig:rq4curves}). Yet held-out fit quality
cannot separate them: vote-weighted RMS between empirical and fitted tie
rates over window$\times$gap-bin cells is $0.0225$ (lattice) versus
$0.0237$ (Davidson), because real matchups concentrate where the shapes
agree. The parameters $\nu$ and $u$ are \emph{not} relabelings of one
quantity---they carry different shape content---but this dataset cannot
say which shape is right. That is a more informative conclusion than
``equivalent,'' and we state it as such.

\centering
\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/fig_rq4_traj.pdf}
\caption{RQ4 drift trajectories (first-class output). \textbf{(a)}
Per-window profiled tie parameters: Davidson $\hat\nu$ rises

\textbf{Tie propensity drifts, and both parameterizations track it.}
Figure~\ref{fig:rq4traj} shows the fitted tie parameters rising
near-monotonically across the study period---$\hat\nu: 0.376 \to 0.559$,
$\hat u: 0.594 \to 0.803$---mirroring the drift in raw tie share. Any
single pooled tie parameter therefore averages a moving target, which is
why every experiment fits per window. As an internal-validity check, the
windowed $\hat u$ endpoints reproduce, to $\sim\!0.01$, two estimates
computed independently months earlier by a different procedure (a
one-shot profile fit on the first three months, $0.5855$, and on the
full sample, $0.8002$): three separately implemented estimation paths
agree on the same drifting quantity.

\textbf{The entanglement cost, measured.} Section~\ref{sec:models}
established the structural asymmetry: Davidson's $\nu$ never touches its
decisive link (Proposition~\ref{prop:davidson}); the lattice's $u$
controls tie mass and decisive steepness jointly. The both-bad robustness
variant turns this into a measurement. Forcing both models to absorb
both-bad-scale tie mass ($\sim\!35\%$ of votes) drives the lattice width
to its pre-specified profile-grid ceiling ($1.4$) in $11/13$
windows---not for want of capacity ($u = 1.4$ expresses $40\%$ at-zero
tie mass) but because the narrow overlap band decays too fast to cover
large-gap ties without inflating at-zero mass. The cost lands on the
decisive channel: on identical held-out decisive votes, switching from
quality-tie fits to both-bad fits degrades the lattice's decisive log
loss by $+4.75\times$MPD (mean over the eleven ceiling windows) versus
$+2.50\times$MPD for the Davidson control---which isolates the shared
effect of refitting abilities under the changed outcome mix, since its
decisive link provably cannot move. The lattice-specific excess,
$\approx\!+2.2\times$MPD, is the measured price of entanglement:
\emph{in a steep-link family with one width parameter, tie mass is
bought with decisive discrimination}. Qualifications: the diagnostic is
modest, heterogeneous across windows, and the grid ceiling was
pre-specified but never stress-tested at both-bad scale; and
independently of this result, ``both bad'' fails the dead-heat
construction conceptually---closeness of draws and a shared absolute bar
are different events---which is why the category was excluded from the
main analysis in the first place.

\section{Discussion}\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{One mechanism, two faces}

The recurring structural actor in our results is the interaction of link
steepness with parameter uncertainty, and it cuts in both directions.
\emph{Prediction-side overconfidence}: a steeper link maps ability-gap
noise into larger probability errors, so plugin predictions from noisy
estimates are overconfident---strong enough in high-noise synthetic
worlds to make the \emph{true} steep model lose held-out to misspecified
BT ($-1.41\times$MPD), measurably absent at our data's pooled noise
levels, and directionally visible (though not cleanly monotone) in the
sub-practical BT leans of Section~\ref{sec:rq3}.
\emph{Estimation-side shrinkage}: the same steepness means the likelihood
needs smaller gaps to explain a given record, so near-zero-data models
are implicitly regularized toward the field---which prevented the
cold-start episode's $\approx 68$-nat loss exactly once in our data.
Which face dominates is a property of the deployment's noise regime, not
of the link: a system with mature, well-sampled models pays the
overconfidence tax for no benefit, while a system continually flooded
with cold-start entrants gets shrinkage protection precisely where BT is
most fragile. For reward modeling and ranking systems generally, the
portable lesson is that \emph{link steepness is an implicit uncertainty
policy}: choosing a steeper-than-necessary link without uncertainty
correction silently trades calibration for shrinkage, and the trade
should be made deliberately---by explicit regularization or
posterior-predictive prediction---rather than inherited from a link
shape.

\subsection{What binds ranking quality here}

Every between-method difference we measured is bounded by fractions of a
threshold that was itself derived from a $10$-Elo reading error, while
three measured quantities tower over all of them: shared underconfidence
on next-month votes (recalibration slope $1.455$ for every model), a
quality-tie share that drifts by half again over sixteen months
($13.1\% \to 20.45\%$, dragging both tie parameters with it), and
$24.8$--$75.8\%$ of next-month decisive votes involving models no
ability model can score. A leaderboard operator's leverage, in order:
get votes onto new models faster; recalibrate for drift; and only then
worry about the link---at which point Proposition~\ref{prop:davidson}
argues for tie mechanisms that leave the decisive link alone.

\subsection{Verdicts hostage to episodes}

Both calibration comparisons ended inconclusive because of single-window
episodes---different ones, through different channels
(cold-start/decisive in RQ3; tie-shape/near-peer in RQ4), in the same
calendar month, adjacent to one of the two unexplained additivity-excess
eras. We resist connecting these dots causally; what they jointly
demonstrate is a methodological point we did not anticipate when
pre-registering: when true effect ceilings sit far below the practical
threshold, month-scale evaluation verdicts are determined by individual
episodes, and any evaluation that does not localize its verdict to its
driving episodes will mistake anecdote for systematic difference---in
either direction.

\section{Limitations and open questions}\label{sec:limits}

\textbf{Scope.} Nothing here tests the multi-entrant field-coherence
property of \citet{cotton2021} (Section~\ref{sec:related}); our conclusions
concern gap-link shape and tie mechanics on pairwise data from one
platform and era. The dose-response evidence for prediction-side
overconfidence is suggestive, not monotone; the shrinkage evidence is a
single mechanistically explained episode. The entanglement measurement
is qualified by its never-stress-tested grid boundary. One pre-registered
design (an entry-proximity event study for context effects) was descoped
rather than run, for the stated reason that the phenomenon it would
instrument proved anti-correlated with entries; and one validation
snapshot (2024-04-03, MAE $2.26$) remains an unexplained pipeline
outlier.

\textbf{Open questions.} The additivity excess
(Section~\ref{sec:excess}) is the dataset's one genuinely unexplained
signal: temporally localized, link-invariant, and unattributed after a
systematic covariate sweep. Distinguishing judge-composition shifts from
genuine context effects requires per-judge panel structure or listwise
data---the same data that would let Cotton's field coherence be tested
directly. Both questions point at the same gap in what preference
platforms currently release.

\section{Conclusion}

We set out to measure whether a theoretically better-motivated link
function improves a real preference leaderboard, and instrumented the
question carefully enough to trust the answer: it does not---not for
refit stability (a dead-zero null), not for link shape (two independent
tests, one ceiling), and not demonstrably for calibration or tie
handling (inconclusive by episode, equivalent-with-a-lean away from
episodes, and contradicted in theory's own best-case location). The
value of the exercise is what the null contains: a provable
orthogonality/entanglement asymmetry between tie mechanisms with a
measured cost; a two-faced steepness--uncertainty mechanism with one
protective episode on record; a quantified account of what actually
binds ranking quality at deployment scale; and one honest anomaly. We
offer the pre-registration protocol itself---ceilings before fitting,
gates before data, verdict criteria before outcomes---as the reason
these negative results are informative rather than merely disappointing.
